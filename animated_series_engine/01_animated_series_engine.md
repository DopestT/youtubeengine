# 01 — Animated Series Engine

The flexible framework. Every concept you test plugs into these ten modules. Keep the
modules fixed; swap the *contents* per concept. That's what makes it repeatable.

---

## Module 1 — Core Premise Structure

A series-ready premise has five locked parts. If any one is missing, the concept will
stall by episode 10.

1. **The Character** — one protagonist with a single dominant trait (e.g. "too honest,"
   "wants to be loved," "thinks they're a genius"). The trait is the engine of every joke.
2. **The Want** — what the character chases in basically every episode (status, love,
   revenge, a sandwich). Small and concrete beats big and abstract.
3. **The Flaw** — the internal thing that guarantees they sabotage the want. This is the
   emotional hook and the comedy source at the same time.
4. **The Arena** — a single, visually distinct place/world the series mostly lives in.
   One arena = cheaper, faster, more recognizable.
5. **The Engine Sentence** — fill this in:
   > *"[Character], who [dominant trait], wants [the want], but [the flaw], so every
   > episode they [recurring action] — and it goes wrong."*

If you can say the engine sentence out loud and immediately imagine 20 episodes, the
premise is strong.

---

## Module 2 — Episode Formula (30–90s)

Use this beat sheet for every episode. It's tuned for the swipe economy: hook in 1s,
payoff before they leave.

| Beat | Time | Job |
|------|------|-----|
| **Cold open hook** | 0–2s | Visual or line that creates an instant question. No logo, no intro. |
| **Setup** | 2–10s | Establish the want for *this* episode in one clear line. |
| **Escalation 1** | 10–25s | Character pursues the want; first complication. |
| **Escalation 2** | 25–45s | Stakes rise; the flaw kicks in. |
| **Turn** | 45–60s | The plan breaks because of the flaw, not bad luck. |
| **Punchline / button** | 60–80s | The biggest laugh or twist lands here. |
| **Tag** | last 2–5s | A tiny extra beat + a repeatable catchphrase/visual signature. |

Rules:
- **One want per episode.** No subplots in Shorts.
- **The flaw, not chance, causes the failure.** That's what makes it a character series.
- **End on the signature.** A repeated final image/sound trains the algorithm and the
  audience to recognize you (this is your "brand stamp").

## Module 3 — Character Archetype System

Build your cast by mixing one **lead** with 2–3 **satellites**. Satellites exist to
bounce the lead's flaw back at them.

**Lead archetypes (pick 1):**
- *The Striver* — desperately wants up the ladder; ego writes checks reality won't cash.
- *The Innocent* — pure, literal, doesn't get how the world works; chaos follows.
- *The Schemer* — always running a plan; the plan always has one fatal hole.
- *The Burnout* — competent but can't be bothered; comedy from minimum effort.
- *The True Believer* — obsessed with one belief/cause everyone else finds insane.

**Satellite archetypes (pick 2–3):**
- *The Straight Man* — reacts so the audience knows what's absurd.
- *The Enabler* — says "yes, and" to the lead's worst ideas.
- *The Rival* — wants the same thing the lead wants, but is better at it.
- *The Wildcard* — unpredictable; used to inject a pattern-interrupt mid-episode.
- *The Anchor* — the one character who actually cares about the lead (emotional core).

**Casting rule:** Each character must be identifiable in **silhouette + one color + one
voice tic**. If you can't tell them apart with the sound off, redesign.

---

## Module 4 — Recurring Conflict System

A series needs conflict that regenerates infinitely without new explanation. Pick a
**primary** engine and a **secondary** for variety.

- **Want vs. Flaw** (internal) — the default; the character is their own villain.
- **Lead vs. Rival** (interpersonal) — a recurring antagonist who mirrors the lead.
- **Character vs. The Rules of the World** (systemic) — the world has one absurd law the
  character keeps crashing into.
- **Character vs. Authority** (power) — a boss/parent/institution figure who blocks them.
- **Order vs. Chaos** (the Wildcard) — one character reliably detonates the plan.

The best engines pair **internal (Want vs. Flaw)** with **one external** so episodes
feel varied but the theme stays consistent.

**Recurring villain design:** the strongest "villain" in Shorts is not evil — it's a
character who is *correct* and *annoying*, or who wants the same thing and is just
better. Pure-evil villains are expensive to write and hard to make funny repeatedly.

---

## Module 5 — World Rules

Define 3–5 fixed laws of the world. They make the comedy consistent and give writers
guardrails (and they're great for fan engagement — audiences love "the rules").

Template:
1. **The One Weird Law** — the single absurd rule everything bends around.
2. **What's normal here that isn't normal to us** — the world's casual reality.
3. **What's forbidden / has consequences** — creates stakes cheaply.
4. **The status system** — how characters measure winning (money, points, rank, likes).
5. **The reset rule** — how the world returns to baseline each episode so it stays
   episodic (important: Shorts viewers arrive in any order).

Keep it small. A world with 4 clear rules is more producible than one with 40.

## Module 6 — Tone Options

Pick a primary tone and a "spice." Stay in lane — tonal drift kills a young channel.

| Tone | Feel | Best for |
|------|------|----------|
| **Warm-absurd** | Silly but kind; nobody's cruel. | Broadest reach, merch, family-adjacent. |
| **Dry / deadpan** | Understated, witty. | Older skew, high rewatch, quotability. |
| **Chaotic** | Fast, loud, escalating. | Max retention, younger skew. |
| **Satirical** | Pointed, topical. | Strong shares, riskier longevity. |
| **Heart-forward** | Comedy with a lump in the throat. | Loyalty, IP depth, longer episodes later. |

**Spice (secondary):** pick one — *cute*, *edgy*, *nerdy*, *wholesome-gross*, *surreal*.

---

## Module 7 — Visual Style Options

Choose for **recognizability + AI-producibility**, not for maximum detail. Simpler
styles are faster, cheaper, more consistent across episodes, and read better at phone size.

- **Flat 2D / thick-line** — bold shapes, limited palette. Fastest, most brandable, most
  meme-able. *Recommended default for AI production.*
- **Cutout / paper / collage** — exaggerated, charming, very cheap to animate.
- **Chunky 3D / claymation-look** — high merch potential, more compute-heavy.
- **Pixel / retro** — built-in nostalgia, niche but loyal.
- **Mixed-media / sticker** — character composited over real footage; very cheap, trendy.

**Brand-stamp checklist (lock these and never change):**
- One signature color the channel owns.
- Character silhouettes that read at 2cm tall.
- One repeated visual motif (a shape, an object, a transition).
- A consistent caption font + placement.

---

## Module 8 — Comedy Style Options

Pick 2 primary mechanisms. Mixing more than 3 makes the voice muddy.

- **Character comedy** — laughs from who they are (most durable; powers IP).
- **Situational / escalation** — small problem snowballs absurdly.
- **Subversion** — set an expectation, break it on the turn.
- **Running gags** — same joke, new context (trains audience, boosts rewatch).
- **Cringe / secondhand embarrassment** — high engagement, use carefully.
- **Wordplay / deadpan one-liners** — high quotability and shareability.
- **Visual / slapstick** — travels across languages = global reach.

**Repeatability tip:** every concept should have **2–3 "format episodes"** — a template
you can refill forever (e.g. "[Character] tries to [do normal thing]" / "[Character]
explains [topic] and gets it horribly wrong"). Formats are how you produce weekly without
burning out.

---

## Module 9 — Audience Targets

Define a **core** and a **spillover** audience. Pick platform priorities; you'll post
everywhere but optimize for one.

- **Primary demo:** age band + psychographic (e.g. "16–24, meme-native, ironic humor").
- **Spillover demo:** the adjacent group that shares it (e.g. "25–34 nostalgic").
- **Platform priority order** (recommended starting point):
  1. **YouTube Shorts** — best long-term discovery, search, and monetization.
  2. **TikTok** — fastest viral feedback loop; use to test hooks.
  3. **Instagram Reels** — strong for character/aesthetic loyalty + merch.
  4. **X** — clips + community + announcements; lowest production weight.
- **The shared trait:** what every target viewer has in common (this is your real
  audience). Write to *that*, not to a demographic.

---

## Module 10 — Monetization Angles

Design the IP so money has somewhere to go *before* you're big. Layered, roughly in order
of how early they unlock:

1. **Platform payouts** — YT Shorts/Partner, TikTok, Reels bonuses (volume game).
2. **Brand integrations** — character-native ad reads / product cameos once you have reach.
3. **Merch** — character-driven (stickers, tees, plushies, mugs). Needs a strong
   silhouette + catchphrase, which the engine is built to produce.
4. **Digital products** — sticker packs, emotes, wallpapers, a comic/zine.
5. **Longer-form** — compilations, "season" supercuts, 5–10 min episodes, then YT main
   channel monetization.
6. **Licensing / collabs** — once the character is recognizable.
7. **Product tie-ins** — the character endorses/embodies a real product later (this is the
   end-game where the IP carries a product, not the reverse).
8. **Community / membership** — Patreon, channel memberships, early access, name-a-character.

**Monetization design rule:** choose concepts whose character is *inherently merchable* —
a clear face, a repeatable catchphrase, an ownable color, and an emotional hook people
want on a shirt. The engine scores every concept on exactly this in `02`.
